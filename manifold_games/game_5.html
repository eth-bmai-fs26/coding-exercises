Key Idea
Houses on a Space Station — A UV Map onto a Torus

The station is shaped like a torus (donut); a 2D surface living in ℝ³. Engineers address every pod using a flat UV map [0,1]² where both edges wrap around: the left edge glues to the right, the top glues to the bottom. The map φ: [0,1]² → T ⊂ ℝ³ wraps this flat square around the torus. u drives the big rotation around the ring (big circle, radius R = 2); v drives the small rotation around the tube (radius r = 0.7). A pod near (u = 0.01, v = 0.5) and one at (u = 0.99, v = 0.5) are next-door neighbours on the station, even though they look far apart on the flat map.

Key Idea
φ⁻¹ — Recovering (u, v) from a 3D Point on the Torus

Given a 3D point (X, Y, Z) on the station, how do we find its flat address (u, v)? The big-ring angle u is the azimuth of (X, Y) around the z-axis: u = atan2(Y, X) / (2π). For the tube angle v, first compute the radial offset ρ = √(X²+Y²) − R — this is how far the point sits from the ring centre. Then v = atan2(Z, ρ) / (2π). Both atan2 calls recover a full 360° angle, giving a unique (u, v) ∈ [0,1]².

Key Insight Unlocked — The Inverse Map φ⁻¹
Recovering u (big angle)
u = atan2(Y, X) / (2π)
u encodes which sector around the ring the pod is in. atan2 recovers the full azimuth from (X, Y) without ambiguity.
Recovering v (tube angle)
ρ = √(X²+Y²) − R
v = atan2(Z, ρ) / (2π)
ρ is the signed radial offset from the ring centre. atan2(Z, ρ) gives the full tube angle across all four quadrants.
Full inverse
φ⁻¹(X,Y,Z) =
(atan2(Y,X)/(2π),
 atan2(Z, ρ−R)/(2π))
Both angles are mapped mod 1 into [0,1]. Two atan2 calls uniquely recover the flat UV address from any point on the torus.
