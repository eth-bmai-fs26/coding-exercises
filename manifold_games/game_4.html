The Cone Hill

Key Idea
Houses on a Town Hill — One Road Address Per House

Now we look at houses on a hill. The town is built on a cone-shaped hill with a single winding road that spirals from the wide base up to the sharp summit. Every house sits somewhere along this 1D road M ⊂ ℝ³ and needs only one number t to describe its location. The map φ: ℝ → M takes a road address t and places the house in 3D space on the hillside. No flat map needed; a single coordinate along the road is enough. Think of t as a house number, if house numbers were a continuous variable.

Key Idea
φ⁻¹ — Reading the Address Back from Height

Given a 3D point (X, Y, Z) on the cone road, can we recover t? The coordinates X and Y each combine a shrinking radius with a periodic trig function. Neither climbs steadily as t grows, they loop and shrink together. But the height Z = t / 4π increases monotonically from 0 at the base to 1 at the apex. It is a perfect, unambiguous address label. So φ⁻¹ simply reads off Z and scales back: t = Z × 4π.

Key Insight Unlocked — The Inverse Map φ⁻¹
Why Z works
Z = t / 4π
⟹ t = Z × 4π
Height Z climbs monotonically from the base (Z = 0) to the apex (Z = 1). It uniquely encodes the road address t with zero ambiguity.
Why X and Y fail alone
X = (1−t/4π)·cos t
Y = (1−t/4π)·sin t
Both combine a shrinking radius with a periodic trig function. Neither is monotone in t, so a given X or Y value does not uniquely identify the address.
The inverse formula
φ⁻¹(X, Y, Z)
= Z × 4π
Only height Z is needed. X and Y are redundant for recovering t — the monotone height alone acts as a perfect, unambiguous address label along the road.
