\section{Physical Diffusion and the Ubiquity of Bell-Shaped Distributions}
Many everyday phenomena can be understood as the result of \emph{lots of tiny random influences piling up}.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Dye spreading in water.} Each molecule gets nudged by countless collisions with water molecules. After many nudges, the cloud of dye becomes a smooth blur that expands over time.
  \item \textbf{Heat spreading in a metal rod.} Energy moves around via many microscopic interactions, producing a smooth ``heat profile'' that spreads out.
  \item \textbf{Measurement noise.} When a sensor is affected by many small independent sources of error, the total error often forms a bell-shaped curve.
  \item \textbf{Human height (roughly).} Height reflects many small contributions (genes, nutrition, health). When many small effects add up, the population often looks bell-shaped around a typical value.
\end{itemize}

A useful mental model is: \emph{each outcome is the sum of many small pushes}, and the \emph{sum tends to form a bell-shaped profile}.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent
In what follows, we will call this bell-shaped family the \textbf{normal curve} (in 1D) and the \textbf{normal cloud} (in multiple dimensions).

\section{Predicting probabilities with an integral (1D)}
Suppose a quantity $x$ is described by a \emph{normal curve} centered at $\mu$ with \textbf{spread} $\sigma$ (``sigma'').
The spread $\sigma$ controls how wide the bell is: a larger $\sigma$ means outcomes are more scattered around the center.
A useful rule of thumb is that the interval $[\mu - 2\sigma,\, \mu + 2\sigma]$ captures approximately 95\% of all outcomes.
In $d$ dimensions the analogous statement is that a sphere of radius
\[
r_{95} \;\approx\; \sigma + \sqrt{d}
\]
centered at $\mu$ captures approximately 95\% of the cloud.
The extra $\sqrt{d}$ term reflects the fact that in high dimensions the cloud concentrates on a shell rather than near the center.

\subsection*{Probability of landing in an interval}
If you want the chance that $x$ lands between $a$ and $b$, you compute the \emph{area under the curve} between $a$ and $b$:
\[
\text{Probability}(a \le x \le b)
\;=\;
\int_{a}^{b} \text{(height of the normal curve at }x\text{)}\,dx.
\]

\noindent
Intuition:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The curve is taller where outcomes are more likely.
  \item The integral adds up ``tiny rectangles'' of probability from $a$ to $b$.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
In practice, people often use a lookup table or software for this integral, but conceptually it is just \emph{area under the bell}.

\section{Predicting probabilities with an integral (multiple dimensions)}
Now let $x$ be a \emph{vector} (e.g., a 2D location or a high-dimensional image vector). The normal curve becomes a \emph{normal cloud}: it assigns a \emph{density} to each point in space.

\subsection*{Probability of landing in a region}
If $R$ is a region (like a rectangle, a circle, or any shape), then
\[
\text{Probability}(x \in R)
\;=\;
\int_{x \in R} \text{(density of the normal cloud at }x\text{)}\,dx.
\]

\noindent
Intuition:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Replace ``area under a curve'' with ``volume under a surface'' (or, in higher dimensions, ``mass in a region'').
  \item You add up tiny pieces of probability mass across the region.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{A common special case: inside a ball}
If the cloud is symmetric around the origin (equally spread in every direction), then a very simple question is:
\[
\text{Probability}(\|x\| \le r) \;=\; \int_{\|x\|\le r} \text{density}(x)\,dx.
\]
This is ``how much probability mass is inside a radius-$r$ ball.''

\section{How to simulate samples using uniforms + adding up (intuition)}
To \emph{simulate} outcomes from these bell-shaped dynamics, we need a way to generate the required random noise.
Assume we only have access to a \textbf{uniform random number generator} that returns values in $[-1,1]$.

\subsection*{The add-up trick (simple and intuitive)}
A classic intuition is:
\begin{quote}
\emph{If you add many independent small random numbers, the result starts looking bell-shaped.}
\end{quote}

Each draw $u_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[-1,1]$ has mean $0$.
Mathematics tells us that the sum $S = u_1 + \cdots + u_n$ increasingly resembles a standard bell-shaped noise as $n$ grows, and the rescaled value
\[
z = S \cdot \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{n}}
\]
behaves like a standard bell-shaped noise.
A convenient choice is $n = 12$, giving $z = S/2$.

\noindent
To get a bell-shaped value centered at $\mu$ with spread $\sigma$, use:
\[
x = \mu + \sigma z.
\]

\subsection*{Multidimensional sampling: isotropic cloud}
The simplest multivariate case is an \textbf{isotropic} (equally spread in every direction) normal cloud.
Since there are no correlations between coordinates, the recipe is just:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Generate $d$ independent bell-shaped noises $z_1,\dots,z_d$ (using the add-up trick above).
  \item Assemble them into a vector $z=(z_1,\dots,z_d)$.
  \item Scale and shift: $x = \mu + \sigma z$.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
Each coordinate gets its own independent nudges; the result is a spherical cloud centered at $\mu$.


\section{Now: simulating a diffusion process (forward corrupts, reverse restores)}
A \textbf{diffusion process} (in the generative-modeling sense) is a controlled way to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Forward:} gradually corrupt structured data into pure noise using many small steps.
  \item \textbf{Reverse:} learn to undo those steps, removing noise little-by-little to recover structure.
\end{itemize}

We will keep things intuitive by writing only \textbf{sampling equations} (recipes for generating the next state).

\subsection*{Ingredients}
We choose a schedule of small noise amounts:
\[
\beta_1,\beta_2,\dots,\beta_T \quad \text{(each is small, between 0 and 1)}
\]
and define
\[
\alpha_t = 1-\beta_t.
\]

We also use a source of \textbf{standard noise}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item In 1D: a scalar noise value $\varepsilon$
  \item In $d$ dimensions: a noise vector $\varepsilon$ whose coordinates are independent bell-shaped noises
\end{itemize}
You can simulate $\varepsilon$ using the \textbf{uniform-add-up trick} from the previous section.

\section{Forward process: one-step and multi-step (sampling-only)}
\subsection*{One-step forward (add a small bit of noise)}
Given $x_{t-1}$, generate the next noisy version $x_t$ by:
\[
x_t \;=\; \sqrt{\alpha_t}\,x_{t-1} \;+\; \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon_t,
\]
where $\varepsilon_t$ is freshly sampled standard noise.

\noindent
Intuition:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The factor $\sqrt{\alpha_t}$ slightly fades the old signal.
  \item The new term injects fresh randomness.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Multi-step forward (direct jump to step $t$)}
If you do many small corruptions, you can write $x_t$ directly from the original $x_0$:
\[
x_t \;=\; \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 \;+\; \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon,
\qquad
\bar\alpha_t \;=\; \alpha_1\alpha_2\cdots\alpha_t,
\]
where $\varepsilon$ is standard noise.

\noindent
This says:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a shrinking fraction of the original survives,
  \item the rest becomes noise.
\end{itemize}

\begin{mdframed}[linewidth=1pt, frametitle={\textbf{Proof (for the curious reader)}}]
\textit{(This derivation can be skipped on first reading.)}

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Claim.} Applying the one-step rule $t$ times yields
\[
x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon,
\qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I),
\]
where $\bar\alpha_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t}\alpha_s$.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Derivation by induction.}

\noindent
\emph{Base case ($t=1$).}
By the one-step rule,
\[
x_1 = \sqrt{\alpha_1}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\alpha_1}\,\varepsilon_1.
\]
This matches the claim with $\bar\alpha_1 = \alpha_1$.

\medskip
\noindent
\emph{Inductive step.}
Assume the claim holds at step $t-1$:
\[
x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,\varepsilon',
\qquad \varepsilon' \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I).
\]
Apply the one-step rule to go from $x_{t-1}$ to $x_t$:
\[
x_t
= \sqrt{\alpha_t}\,x_{t-1} + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon_t
= \sqrt{\alpha_t}\!\left(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,\varepsilon'\right) + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon_t.
\]
Collecting the $x_0$ term and the noise terms:
\[
x_t
= \sqrt{\alpha_t\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,x_0
  + \underbrace{\sqrt{\alpha_t(1-\bar\alpha_{t-1})}\,\varepsilon' + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon_t}_{\text{sum of two independent bell-shaped noises}}.
\]
The sum of two independent bell-shaped noises with variances $a^2$ and $b^2$ is itself a bell-shaped noise with variance $a^2+b^2$.
Here $a^2 = \alpha_t(1-\bar\alpha_{t-1})$ and $b^2 = 1-\alpha_t$, so the combined noise has variance
\[
\alpha_t(1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}) + (1-\alpha_t)
= \alpha_t - \alpha_t\bar\alpha_{t-1} + 1 - \alpha_t
= 1 - \alpha_t\bar\alpha_{t-1}
= 1 - \bar\alpha_t.
\]
Therefore
\[
x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon,
\]
which completes the induction. \hfill$\square$
\end{mdframed}

\section{Reverse process (model): one-step and multi-step (sampling-only)}

\subsection*{Step 1: the ideal reverse formula (if we knew $\varepsilon$)}

Recall the multi-step forward formula:
\[
x_t \;=\; \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 \;+\; \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon.
\]
If we happened to know the noise $\varepsilon$ that was used to produce $x_t$, we could solve for $x_0$ exactly:
\[
x_0 \;=\; \frac{x_t - \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}.
\]
Substituting this into the one-step forward rule (run backwards) gives the \emph{ideal} reverse step:
\[
x_{t-1}
\;=\;
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}
\left(
x_t \;-\; \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\,\varepsilon
\right)
\;+\; \sqrt{\beta_t}\,z,
\]
where $z$ is a small fresh noise injection that keeps the step statistically consistent.
This formula is exact: given $\varepsilon$, it recovers $x_{t-1}$ perfectly.

\subsection*{Step 2: the problem — $\varepsilon$ is unknown at inference time}

During generation we start from pure noise and have no access to any real $x_0$, so the noise $\varepsilon$ that ``would have'' produced our $x_t$ is unknown.
We must \emph{estimate} it.

\subsection*{Step 3: learned denoising — replace $\varepsilon$ by $\widehat{\varepsilon}_\theta$}

We train a neural network $\widehat{\varepsilon}_\theta(x_t, t)$ to predict $\varepsilon$ given only $x_t$ and the time step $t$.
Substituting the network's prediction for the unknown $\varepsilon$ gives the formula we actually use:
\[
x_{t-1}
\;=\;
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}
\left(
x_t \;-\; \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}} \cdot \widehat{\varepsilon}_\theta(x_t,t)
\right)
\;+\;
\sqrt{\beta_t}\, z,
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\widehat{\varepsilon}_\theta(x_t,t)$ is the network's estimate of the noise $\varepsilon$ inside $x_t$,
  \item $\frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}$ is a known scalar from the schedule,
  \item $z$ is fresh standard noise (set to $0$ on the final step),
  \item $\sqrt{\beta_t}$ controls the small randomness injected at each reverse step.
\end{itemize}

\begin{mdframed}[linewidth=1pt, frametitle={\textbf{Proof (for the curious reader): derivation of the reverse step}}]
\textit{(This derivation can be skipped on first reading.)}

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Goal.} Show that the reverse step formula follows from the multi-step forward formula and the one-step forward rule.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{1. Recover $x_0$ from $x_t$ and $\varepsilon$.}
The multi-step forward formula states
\[
x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon.
\]
Solving for $x_0$:
\[
x_0 = \frac{x_t - \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}.
\tag{$*$}
\]

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{2. Write $x_{t-1}$ using the multi-step formula at $t-1$.}
Applying the multi-step formula one step earlier:
\[
x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,\varepsilon'.
\]
For the reverse step we want the \emph{mean} of $x_{t-1}$ given $x_t$ (ignoring the residual noise $\varepsilon'$ for the moment), so we substitute $(*)$:
\[
\mathbb{E}[x_{t-1} \mid x_t]
= \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\cdot\frac{x_t - \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}.
\]

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{3. Rewrite using $\alpha_t = \bar\alpha_t / \bar\alpha_{t-1}$.}
Note that $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}/\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t} = 1/\sqrt{\alpha_t}$.
Expanding:
\[
\mathbb{E}[x_{t-1} \mid x_t]
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\left(x_t - \frac{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\,\cdot\,\frac{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}}}\,\varepsilon\right)
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\left(x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\,\varepsilon\right),
\]
where the last equality uses $\beta_t = 1-\alpha_t$ and the algebraic identity
\[
\frac{1-\bar\alpha_t}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} - \frac{\sqrt{(1-\bar\alpha_t)(1-\bar\alpha_{t-1})}}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t/\bar\alpha_{t-1}}}
\;=\; \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}.
\]

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{4. Add back the residual randomness.}
The true posterior of $x_{t-1}$ given $x_t$ and $x_0$ is a bell-shaped cloud centred at the mean above, with variance $\beta_t$ (the DDPM simplest choice).
Adding the residual noise $\sqrt{\beta_t}\,z$, $z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$, gives the full reverse sampling formula:
\[
x_{t-1}
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\!\left(x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\,\varepsilon\right) + \sqrt{\beta_t}\,z.
\]
Replacing the unknown $\varepsilon$ by its network estimate $\widehat\varepsilon_\theta(x_t,t)$ gives the formula used in practice. \hfill$\square$
\end{mdframed}

\subsection*{Multi-step reverse (the full generation procedure)}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Start from pure noise: sample $x_T$ as standard noise.
  \item For $t=T, T-1, \dots, 1$:
  \[
  x_{t-1}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}
  \left(
  x_t \;-\; \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}} \cdot \widehat{\varepsilon}_\theta(x_t,t)
  \right)
  \;+\;
  \sqrt{\beta_t}\, z.
  \]
  \item Output $x_0$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion: how training works (the key idea)}
We train the diffusion model by repeatedly:
\begin{itemize}
  \item taking a real object $x_0$,
  \item corrupting it to some step $t$ using the forward sampling rule,
  \item and teaching a neural network to \textbf{predict the noise} that was used.
\end{itemize}

In other words:
\begin{quote}
\emph{The network learns to estimate ``how much noise is inside this noisy object at step $t$,''}
and that estimate is exactly what the reverse sampling step needs in order to denoise.
\end{quote}
