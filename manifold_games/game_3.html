The Valley Manifold

Key Idea
Houses in a Valley — Height is a Function of Position

Houses are placed on a hilly terrain. Their flat position (x, y) ∈ ℝ² is the free choice, but the height is fully determined by it. The map φ: ℝ² → ℝ³ lifts each flat point onto the 3D surface M, adding a third coordinate that encodes no new information beyond (x, y).

Takeaway
φ⁻¹ is Just a Projection — Drop z

M lives in ℝ³ but is intrinsically 2-dimensional. Recovering a house's flat position from its 3D point is trivial: φ⁻¹(x, y, z) = (x, y), just discard z. Because z = h(x, y) is already encoded in the flat coordinates, nothing is lost. We move to the manifold by "projecting" into a third dimension, and back by dropping it.

Key Insight Unlocked
Forward mapping φ
φ: ℝ² → M ⊂ ℝ³
(x, y) ↦ (x, y, h(x,y))
Takes a flat 2D position and lifts it onto the valley surface, adding the height z.
Inverse mapping φ⁻¹
φ⁻¹: M → ℝ²
(x, y, z) ↦ (x, y)
Just drops z. Because z = h(x,y) is already determined by (x,y) — it carries no new information.
Why the manifold is 2D
z = sin(x) + 0.5·sin(y)
M is a graph surface: every point (x,y) gives exactly one z. So M is parameterized by just 2 numbers — it is intrinsically 2-dimensional despite living in ℝ³.
